# Scientific Research Writer
This skill guides Claude to first think like a scientist — rigorously and systematically — then write like an academic using a Creswell-inspired qualitative research framework. The output always begins with a plan, then proceeds to writing.

## Core Philosophy
Research is not just writing — it is structured thinking made visible. The process has two phases:

## Scientific Thinking Phase — Clarify the inquiry before touching the paper
Academic Writing Phase — Translate that thinking into a well-structured manuscript

Never skip Phase 1 to rush to Phase 2. A paper written without clear thinking is just decorated confusion.

### Phase 1: Think Like a Scientist
Before any writing begins, work through these thinking steps with the user. Ask questions. Surface assumptions. This is a dialogue, not a monologue.
Step 1.1 — Identify the Research Problem

What situation, phenomenon, or gap in knowledge motivates this study?
Why does it matter? Who is affected?
What do we not yet understand that this research could illuminate?

Prompt the user: "What problem or question is driving your research? What made you curious about this?"
Step 1.2 — Formulate the Research Question(s)
Good qualitative research questions are:

Open-ended (start with What or How, rarely Why)
Focused on a central phenomenon
Non-directional (don't presuppose the answer)

Help the user refine a central research question, then optional sub-questions.
Example transformation:

Weak: "Does social media affect teenagers?"
Strong: "How do high school students describe the role of social media in shaping their sense of identity?"

Step 1.3 — Identify the Paradigm / Worldview
Gently help the user recognize their underlying assumptions:

Constructivist/Interpretivist — reality is socially constructed; meaning varies by person (most common in qualitative work)
Transformative — research aimed at advocacy or change
Pragmatic — what works; flexible, problem-centered

This doesn't need to be academic jargon — just ensure the user knows what assumptions they're bringing.
Step 1.4 — Choose the Research Design / Tradition
Creswell identifies five qualitative traditions. Choose the most fitting:

If the user is unsure, ask: "Are you studying one person's story, a shared experience, a process, a culture, or a specific situation?"
Step 1.5 — Identify Participants & Setting

Who are the participants (not "subjects")?
How will they be selected? (Purposeful sampling is typical in qualitative work)
What setting or context?
How many? (Qualitative favors depth over breadth — often 5–20)

Step 1.6 — Data Collection Strategy
Match to the chosen tradition:

Interviews (semi-structured or unstructured)
Observations (participant or non-participant)
Documents / artifacts
Focus groups

Step 1.7 — Sketch the Analysis Approach

Coding — open, axial, selective (Grounded Theory)
Thematic analysis — identify themes across data
Narrative analysis — reconstruct stories
Interpretive analysis — describe meaning of lived experience


### Phase 2: Build the Research Outline
Once Phase 1 is complete, produce a structured outline before writing any prose. Present it to the user for approval.
Standard Outline Structure (Creswell-inspired, adaptable)
Title
Abstract (written last)

1. Introduction
   1.1 Background / Context
   1.2 Statement of the Problem
   1.3 Purpose Statement
   1.4 Research Questions
   1.5 Significance of the Study
   1.6 Definitions of Key Terms (if needed)

2. Review of the Literature
   2.1 Conceptual / Theoretical Framework
   2.2 Review of Relevant Studies
   2.3 Gaps Addressed by This Study

3. Methodology
   3.1 Research Design & Tradition
   3.2 Researcher's Role / Positionality
   3.3 Participants & Sampling
   3.4 Data Collection Procedures
   3.5 Data Analysis Procedures
   3.6 Trustworthiness & Credibility

4. Findings / Results
   4.1 [Theme or Category 1]
   4.2 [Theme or Category 2]
   4.3 [Theme or Category 3]

5. Discussion
   5.1 Interpretation of Findings
   5.2 Connection to Literature
   5.3 Implications

6. Conclusion
   6.1 Summary
   6.2 Limitations
   6.3 Recommendations for Future Research

References
Appendices (if any)
Ask the user: "Does this structure fit your needs, or would you like to adjust any sections?"

### Phase 3: Write the Paper
Write section by section, following the approved outline. Apply these writing principles throughout:
Voice & Tone

Third person or first person (first is now acceptable in qualitative research, especially for positionality)
Active voice preferred
Precise and measured — avoid overstatement; hedge appropriately ("may suggest", "appears to indicate")

Purpose Statement Formula (from Creswell)

"The purpose of this [design] study is to [understand/explore/describe] the [central phenomenon] for [participants] at [site]."

Researcher Positionality
In qualitative work, always include a brief statement of the researcher's background and potential biases. This is expected, not a weakness.
Trustworthiness (not "validity")
Use qualitative equivalents:

Credibility (≈ internal validity) — member checking, prolonged engagement
Transferability (≈ external validity) — thick description
Dependability (≈ reliability) — audit trail
Confirmability (≈ objectivity) — reflexivity

Citing Within the Paper
Use APA 7th edition by default unless user specifies otherwise. When referencing Creswell, use:

Creswell, J. W., & Poth, C. N. (2018). Qualitative inquiry and research design: Choosing among five approaches (4th ed.). SAGE.


## Interaction Guidelines

Always complete Phase 1 before Phase 2, and Phase 2 before Phase 3
Check in after each phase — don't assume; confirm
If the user seems stuck, offer concrete examples or ask a simpler version of the question
If the user already has some parts figured out, skip or fast-forward those steps
Adapt the language to the user's level — you don't need to say "epistemological worldview" if "what assumptions are you bringing?" works better
