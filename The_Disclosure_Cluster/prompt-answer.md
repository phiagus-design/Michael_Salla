Good — this is exactly the right starting point: you've named a topic ("the disclosure cluster") but not yet a question or a method, so let's do the triage before any writing happens.

One thing worth naming up front, because it shapes everything downstream: "the disclosure cluster" is a set of *claims and events* (the post-2016 spike — VIP Antarctic visits, the Spartan testimonies, Space Force, the ark "activations," the 2022 Congressional hearing, the remote-viewing study). A rigorous study doesn't have to take those claims as true or false. The legitimate, researchable object is the **disclosure phenomenon itself** — the testimonies, the narratives, the people advancing and believing them, the media dynamics, and how the exopolitics version relates to the real, documented government UAP disclosures of the same period. That stance (studying the phenomenon, bracketing the truth-claims) is what keeps it credible social science rather than advocacy.

So before I can map your topic against the three methodologies, I need to know what you actually want to *find out* — because the method has to follow the question, never the reverse.

That points clearly in one direction already, but let me confirm the one thing that determines whether the study is even buildable: the data.

A "why did it surge" question answered through *meaning and themes* is a qualitative inquiry — you're reconstructing the forces and motivations behind a wave of disclosure, not measuring them. The catch is that "why" is the hardest thing to get at qualitatively, and how you get at it depends entirely on what you can actually lay hands on. For your specific topic the realistic sources are very different from each other: the published corpus (Salla's books, exopolitics.org articles, the recorded "JP" interviews, whistleblower video testimony) is enormous and fully accessible to you right now; the *people* (the whistleblowers, the audience, the journalists who amplified it) are far harder to reach and would raise access and ethics hurdles.

So:

> Q: Realistically, what data can you get your hands on for the 'why'?
> A: Mostly documents/recordings I can collect (books, articles, videos, hearings)

That settles it. All three diagnostics now point the same way — a *why/meaning* question (D1), an area with no established academic instruments (D2), and a corpus of documents and recordings rather than reachable people (D3). But before I name the recommendation, here's your actual topic refracted through all three methodologies, so the choice is yours and not just mine.

**As a qualitative study.** The research question becomes something like: *"What drivers and triggers does the post-2016 disclosure wave invoke, and how did its narrative shift across that period?"* The natural traditions for a document corpus are qualitative content/thematic analysis, a single **case study** of the disclosure movement, or a **narrative/discourse analysis** of how the story is told. You'd assemble the corpus (Salla's later books, exopolitics.org pieces, the JP interviews, whistleblower videos) and read it against the real-world backdrop of the same years — the 2017 *NYT*/AATIP story, the 2021 ODNI report, the 2022 hearing — coding for the recurring drivers actors themselves articulate and watching how the framing evolves. What it reveals: the *meaning structure* of the surge — why insiders and believers say it happened, which rhetorical and contextual triggers keep recurring, how the movement positioned itself against official disclosure. Its blind spot: it can show you the **claimed and narrated** causes, not prove they're the **actual** causes, and it won't generalize.

**As a quantitative study.** You'd recast "disclosure" as a countable variable — say, disclosure articles/videos/testimonies per quarter — and test whether the post-2016 rise is statistically real and which external signals it tracks: mainstream UAP news volume, Google Trends, the timing of Pentagon/Congressional UAP events. Design: descriptive time-series plus correlational content analysis with a coding scheme. What it measures: the *shape* of the surge and its co-movement with candidate triggers. Its blind spot: it strips out the "why" entirely — volumes correlating with volumes tells you nothing about motivation or texture — and your corpus isn't a clean random sample, so the inference is shaky.

**As a mixed methods study.** Explanatory-sequential: quantify the surge and its correlates first, then explain the *why* through thematic or narrative analysis of the documents. Integration would show both that it spiked / what it tracked *and* the meaning underneath. The cost: roughly double the work and two skillsets, and on a non-random corpus the thin quantitative strand risks wagging the rich qualitative dog.

**My recommendation: qualitative — specifically a document-based design.** Every diagnostic converges there. Your question is squarely a "why does this mean what it means" question; the terrain is exploratory enough that there's nothing validated to measure against; and your data is a textual/recorded corpus with no human subjects, which is the home ground of qualitative document analysis. The only open choice left is *which* qualitative tradition — and it's a real choice: a **case study** if you want to treat the post-2016 wave as one bounded phenomenon and explain it in the round; a **thematic/qualitative content analysis** if you want disciplined coding of recurring drivers across the corpus; or a **discourse/narrative** lens if you think the interesting thing is how the surge was *constructed and told*. My lean is a case study with a discourse-analytic edge, but that's genuinely yours to set.
